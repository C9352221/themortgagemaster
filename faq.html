Frequently Asked Questions.

Buying a home, refinancing, or unlocking equity raises a lot of questions — and the answers shape some of the biggest financial decisions you'll ever make. Here are honest answers to the questions clients ask most.

Buying a Home

What credit score do I need to buy a home?

Different programs have different floors. Conventional loans typically look for 620+, FHA can work down to 580 (and sometimes lower with compensating factors), and VA loans don't have a hard minimum but most lenders want around 580–620. The score is only one input — debt-to-income, savings, and employment history all matter too. If you're unsure, send me your scenario and I'll tell you exactly where you stand.

How much do I really need for a down payment?

Less than most people think. First-time buyer programs can get you in with 3% down on conventional loans, FHA is 3.5% down, and VA loans for eligible veterans are 0% down. Florida also has down-payment assistance programs that can stack on top — I'll match you to whatever you qualify for.

What's the difference between pre-qualification and pre-approval?

Pre-qualification is a quick, informal estimate based on what you tell me — useful for early conversations. Pre-approval is the real thing: I pull credit, review documents, and issue a letter that sellers and listing agents take seriously. In a competitive market, pre-approval is what gets your offer to the top of the pile.

Can I use gift funds for my down payment?

Yes — most loan programs allow gift funds from a family member, with proper documentation (a gift letter and proof of transfer). FHA, VA, and conventional all accept gifts; the specifics depend on the program and your overall scenario.

Are there special programs for first-time buyers in Florida?

Florida has several down-payment assistance and bond programs that can pair with a conventional or FHA first mortgage. Eligibility depends on income, purchase price, and the county you're buying in. I'll check what you qualify for and run the math both ways so you can see whether the assistance actually saves you money over the life of the loan.

Refinancing

When does it make sense to refinance?

The classic case is when current rates are meaningfully lower than your existing rate and you plan to stay in the home long enough to recoup the closing costs (your "break-even"). It can also make sense to refinance to get rid of mortgage insurance, switch from an ARM to a fixed rate, or pull cash out at a better blended rate than your other debt. I'll run the math with you before we file anything — if it doesn't pencil, I'll tell you.

What's the difference between rate-and-term and cash-out refinance?

Rate-and-term replaces your existing loan with a new one at a different rate or term — no money out of pocket to you, no money pulled from equity. Cash-out lets you borrow against the equity you've built and walk away with funds (for renovations, debt consolidation, an investment, etc.). Cash-out usually carries a slightly higher rate because of the added risk to the lender.

What's an FHA Streamline or VA IRRRL?

Both are simplified refinance options for borrowers who already have an FHA or VA loan. They typically skip the appraisal, require less documentation, and can close faster than a standard refinance. Available when the new loan provides a clear benefit to you — usually a lower rate or payment.

How long does a refinance take?

Typically a few weeks from start to close, depending on the program, the appraisal, and how quickly we get documentation in. Streamline refinances are usually faster than full refinances. Every scenario is different — I'll give you a realistic timeline once I see your file.

VA Loans & Veterans

Who's eligible for a VA loan?

Eligible veterans, active-duty service members, certain reservists and National Guard members, and some surviving spouses qualify. Eligibility is verified by a Certificate of Eligibility (COE) from the VA — I help clients pull this as the first step.

What does $0 down really mean — are there hidden costs?

VA loans don't require a down payment, and they don't carry monthly mortgage insurance (PMI). There is a one-time VA funding fee, which can usually be rolled into the loan. Closing costs still exist, but veterans often have flexibility around who pays them. Net result: VA is one of the most generous loan programs available, and for most eligible borrowers it's the best option on the market.

Can I use my VA benefit more than once?

Yes — the VA benefit is a lifetime benefit. You can use it again after paying off a previous VA loan, and in some cases you can have more than one VA loan at the same time depending on your remaining entitlement. I'll calculate your usable entitlement and walk you through the options.

What's a VA IRRRL?

The Interest Rate Reduction Refinance Loan is a streamlined VA refinance for borrowers who already have a VA loan and want to lower their rate or payment. It's faster and lighter on documentation than a standard refinance, and it doesn't typically require a new appraisal.

Investment Properties

What's a DSCR loan and when should I consider one?

DSCR (Debt Service Coverage Ratio) loans qualify the borrower based on the property's rental income rather than the borrower's personal W-2 income. Useful for investors who own multiple properties, are self-employed, or have complex tax returns that make conventional underwriting tough. Rates are usually higher than conventional, and the structure is different — I'll show you both side by side so you can see which fits.

Can I finance a 2–4 unit property?

Yes. Conventional and FHA both allow 2–4 unit properties. If you live in one of the units, you may qualify for owner-occupied terms (better rates, lower down payment) — this is one of the most underrated wealth-building strategies for new investors. We can also structure it as a pure investment if that fits your plan better.

How is investment property financing different from primary residence financing?

Investment loans typically require a larger down payment, carry slightly higher rates, and have stricter reserve requirements (months of payments held in savings). Underwriting also looks more closely at rental income and your overall portfolio. The good news: as a broker with access to over 700 lenders, I can often find a structure that conventional retail bank channels can't.

The Process & Timeline

How long does the process take from application to closing?

A typical purchase closes in roughly 30–45 days from contract, but it depends on the program, the property, and how quickly documentation comes in. Refinances often close faster, especially streamlines. I'll give you a realistic timeline once I see your scenario, and I keep clients updated at every step.

What documents will I need to gather?

The standard list: two years of W-2s or tax returns, recent pay stubs, two months of bank statements, a copy of your ID, and details on any other properties you own. For self-employed borrowers there's typically more, and for VA loans we'll also need your Certificate of Eligibility. I send a clean checklist as soon as we kick off, so nothing surprises you.

What is a rate lock and when should I lock?

A rate lock guarantees your interest rate for a set period (commonly 30–60 days) so the rate can't move up while you're closing. The right time to lock depends on market conditions and where you are in the process — I'll give you my honest read when we get to that point.

What are closing costs and who pays them?

Closing costs cover lender fees, title insurance, appraisal, taxes, and prepaid escrows for insurance and property taxes. They typically run a few percent of the loan amount, varying by program and county. Depending on the structure of your offer or refinance, you may pay them out of pocket, roll them into the loan, or have a portion covered by the seller or lender credit. I'll show you a clear breakdown before you commit to anything.

Working With a Broker vs a Bank

Why work with a mortgage broker instead of going to my bank?

A bank offers you the bank's products. A broker shops your scenario across the entire wholesale market — in my case, over 700 lenders — and finds the loan that actually fits your situation. Different lenders price the same scenario differently, and they specialize in different niches. The result: most clients end up with a better rate, a better structure, or both, than they would have gotten at a single retail bank.

Does going through a broker hurt my credit score?

No. When I shop your scenario across multiple lenders, the credit bureaus treat all the inquiries within a short window as a single inquiry for scoring purposes. You don't get penalized for letting me find you the best deal.

What does it cost to work with you?

You don't pay me out of pocket. I'm compensated through the loan transaction itself — the same way every loan officer is, whether at a bank or a brokerage. The difference is that as a broker I'm working for you, not for one bank's product list. My only goal is to find your best deal — that's how I earn the right to your next referral.
